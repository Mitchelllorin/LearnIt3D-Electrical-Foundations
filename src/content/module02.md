Full Lesson Text
Voltage is electrical potential difference, current is charge flow, and resistance opposes current. Ohm’s Law connects them: V = I × R. Rearranged forms are I = V / R and R = V / I.

Example: a 120 V source across 24 Ω gives current of 5 A. If current rises while voltage stays fixed, resistance must have dropped. If resistance increases (longer wire, higher temperature in many conductors), current falls for the same voltage.

Units matter: volts (V), amperes (A), ohms (Ω). Converting between milli and base units prevents common math errors. Apprentices should also estimate expected ranges before measuring so dangerous or impossible values are recognized immediately.

In troubleshooting, Ohm’s Law helps predict behavior. If a load is drawing too much current, check for reduced resistance, shorted paths, or incorrect wiring. If current is too low, check open connections, high-resistance joints, and supply voltage drop.
