## COMPLETE ANSWER KEY

### Section A: Multiple Choice

1. **B — 60 cycles per second**  
   Hertz is the unit of frequency, and 60 Hz means the waveform completes 60 cycles each second.

2. **B — The effective heating value compared with DC**  
   RMS is used because it tells us the equivalent DC value that would produce the same heating effect in a resistive load.

3. **B — Battery**  
   Batteries are common DC sources because their polarity stays constant.

4. **C — Can be transformed easily to different voltages**  
   This is a major reason AC is efficient and practical for transmission and distribution.

5. **B — Electromagnetic induction**  
   Transformers transfer energy between windings through a changing magnetic field.

6. **B — Step-down transformer**  
   A step-down transformer lowers voltage from primary to secondary.

7. **B — 120 V**  
   In a standard Canadian split-phase service, each hot leg to neutral is about 120 V.

8. **C — Silver screw**  
   Neutral conductors terminate on the silver-coloured terminal of a standard receptacle.

9. **B — Green or bare copper**  
   These are the usual colours for bonding or equipment grounding conductors.

10. **A — Bathrooms and outdoor receptacles**  
    GFCI protection is used where shock risk is higher because of moisture or contact with ground.

11. **A — Habitable spaces such as bedrooms and living areas**  
    AFCI protection is commonly applied to many dwelling branch circuits in occupied spaces.

12. **A — Sustained overload current**  
    The thermal element reacts to heating caused by overloads over time.

13. **B — Short-circuit current**  
    The magnetic element trips rapidly when fault current rises sharply.

14. **B — Isolated from each other**  
    In a downstream subpanel, neutral and bonding conductors are generally kept separate.

15. **A — Rotating magnetic field**  
    The rotating magnetic field induces rotor current and produces torque.

16. **B — Inductive**  
    Motors are inductive loads because they rely on magnetic fields.

17. **B — Frequency and voltage**  
    A VFD changes motor speed by adjusting the output frequency and corresponding voltage.

18. **B — Identify and verify the symptom**  
    Good troubleshooting begins by confirming what the actual problem is before replacing anything.

19. **B — Short circuit or severe fault**  
    Instantaneous tripping usually points to a major fault, not a normal load condition.

20. **B — Known live source, target circuit, known live source**  
    This live-dead-live method confirms both the target condition and that the meter is functioning properly.

### Section B: Calculation Problems

21. **Vpeak = 169.7 V**  
   Formula: `Vpeak = Vrms × √2`  
   `Vpeak = 120 × 1.414 = 169.68 V`  
   Rounded: **169.7 V peak**

22. **Period = 0.0167 s = 16.7 ms**  
   Formula: `T = 1 / f`  
   `T = 1 / 60 = 0.01667 s`  
   Multiply by 1000 to convert to milliseconds:  
   `0.01667 s = 16.67 ms`  
   **Answer: 0.0167 s or 16.7 ms**

23. **Secondary voltage = 12 V**  
   Formula: `Vs / Vp = Ns / Np`  
   `Vs / 240 = 50 / 1000 = 0.05`  
   `Vs = 240 × 0.05 = 12 V`  
   **Answer: 12 V**

24. **Primary current = 0.4 A**  
   For an ideal transformer, input power is approximately equal to output power:  
   `VpIp = VsIs`

   `Ip = (Vs × Is) / Vp`  
   `Ip = (24 × 4) / 240 = 96 / 240 = 0.4 A`  
   **Answer: 0.4 A**

25. **Total load = 1500 W; Current = 12.5 A**  
   **a. Total wattage**  
   `180 + 1200 + 120 = 1500 W`

   **b. Branch current**  
   `I = P / V = 1500 / 120 = 12.5 A`

   **Answer: 1500 W total and 12.5 A**

26. **Minimum breaker rating = 20 A**  
   Formula: `breaker size = load ÷ 0.8`  
   `16 ÷ 0.8 = 20`  
   **Answer: 20 A breaker**

27. **Motor current = 6.25 A**  
   Formula: `I = P / V`  
   `I = 1500 / 240 = 6.25 A`  
   **Answer: 6.25 A**

28. **Energy = 320 kWh; Cost = $48.00**  
   Daily energy use:  
   `2.0 kW × 8 h = 16 kWh/day`

   Over 20 days:  
   `16 × 20 = 320 kWh`

   Cost:  
   `320 × 0.15 = 48.00`

   **Answer: 320 kWh and $48.00**

29. **Current before correction = 10.42 A; after correction = 8.77 A; reduction = 1.65 A**  
   **a. Before correction**  
   `I = P / (V × PF)`  
   `I = 2000 / (240 × 0.80)`  
   `I = 2000 / 192 = 10.42 A`

   **b. After correction to PF = 0.95**  
   `I = 2000 / (240 × 0.95)`  
   `I = 2000 / 228 = 8.77 A`

   **c. Current reduction**  
   `10.42 - 8.77 = 1.65 A`

   Improving power factor reduces line current for the same real power.  
   **Answer: 10.42 A, 8.77 A, reduction 1.65 A**

30. **Voltage drop = 3.75 V; Percentage drop = 3.1%**  
   **a. Total circuit length**  
   Voltage drop uses the outgoing and return conductors:  
   `30 m × 2 = 60 m`

   **b. Total conductor resistance**  
   `R = 5.21 Ω/1000 m × 60 m`  
   `R = 5.21 × 0.06 = 0.3126 Ω`

   **c. Voltage drop**  
   `Vdrop = I × R = 12 × 0.3126 = 3.7512 V`

   **d. Percentage drop**  
   `% drop = (3.7512 / 120) × 100 = 3.13%`

   Rounded: **3.75 V drop** and **3.1% voltage drop**

### Section C: Troubleshooting Scenarios

31. **Dead Downstream Receptacles**  
   - **Likely fault:** An open connection at the first receptacle, often a loose splice, failed back-stab connection, or open neutral/hot feeding the downstream devices.  
   - **Safe test method:** De-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, remove the first working/dead transition receptacle, and inspect or test continuity of the feed-through connections. Energized voltage checks can also be made carefully at the line and load side after safe access is established.  
   - **Repair:** Re-make the splice or termination correctly, replace any damaged receptacle, and re-test all downstream outlets.

32. **Baseboard Heater Not Heating**  
   - **Likely fault:** One hot leg is open upstream of the heater, often at the thermostat, splice, or breaker connection. Seeing 120 V from each leg to bond but 0 V across the heater indicates both heater terminals are sitting at the same potential instead of being 240 V apart.  
   - **Safe test method:** Measure voltage line-to-line at the thermostat and heater terminals, then de-energize and inspect continuity through the open component or connection.  
   - **Repair:** Tighten or replace the failed splice, thermostat, breaker connection, or conductor so full 240 V is restored across the heater.

33. **Motor Hums and Trips**  
   - **Likely fault:** A failed start capacitor or another starting-circuit problem on a single-phase motor; mechanical seizure is another possible cause.  
   - **Safe test method:** Isolate power, inspect the motor shaft for free rotation, test the capacitor if applicable, and compare winding readings to expected values or the manufacturer information.  
   - **Repair:** Replace the failed start capacitor or defective starting component, or correct the mechanical binding before returning the motor to service.

34. **Lighting Circuit Trips Instantly**  
   - **Likely fault:** A short circuit caused during the new luminaire installation, such as hot-to-neutral or hot-to-bond contact in the fixture box.  
   - **Safe test method:** De-energize the circuit, verify absence of voltage, inspect all luminaire splices and terminations, and test for continuity to ground or neutral where a short is suspected.  
   - **Repair:** Correct the wiring error, re-insulate or re-terminate conductors properly, replace damaged parts if needed, and test the circuit again.

35. **Outdoor Receptacle Fault After Rain**  
   - **Likely fault:** Moisture intrusion causing leakage current to ground and tripping the GFCI.  
   - **Safe test method:** De-energize the circuit, inspect the outdoor box, receptacle, gasket, and cover for water entry, then perform insulation-resistance or visual checks after drying the assembly.  
   - **Repair:** Dry or replace the wet/damaged receptacle, install a proper weather-resistant device and in-use cover if required, reseal the box, and verify normal GFCI operation.

### Section D: Diagram Interpretation

36. **Distribution Transformer Diagram**
   - **a. Step-down** — It reduces utility distribution voltage to usable residential voltage.
   - **b. 120 V** — Either hot leg to neutral is approximately 120 V.
   - **c. 240 V** — Hot leg to hot leg is approximately 240 V.
   - **d. Centre tap purpose:** It provides the neutral point so the system can supply both 120 V loads and 240 V loads.

37. **Panelboard Diagram**
   - **a. The two-pole 30 A breaker** supplies the 240 V water heater.
   - **b. Why two-pole:** A 240 V load must connect to both hot legs of the split-phase system, and the breaker disconnects both ungrounded conductors together.
   - **c. Panel directory purpose:** It clearly identifies what each breaker controls so circuits can be isolated, serviced, and restored safely.

38. **Motor Control Diagram**
   - **a. START pressed:** The normally open START pushbutton closes, energizing coil **M**, which pulls in the contactor.
   - **b. Auxiliary sealing contact:** It closes after the contactor picks up and keeps the coil energized after the START button is released.
   - **c. Overload opens:** The coil circuit is interrupted, the contactor drops out, and the motor stops to protect the motor from sustained overcurrent.

39. **Waveform Diagram**
   - **a. AC** — A sine wave that alternates positive and negative is alternating current.
   - **b. RMS voltage:** `Vrms = Vpeak / √2 = 170 / 1.414 ≈ 120 V`.
   - **c. Period:** At 60 Hz, `T = 1 / 60 = 0.0167 s = 16.7 ms`.

40. **Residential Branch-Circuit Diagram**
   - **a. White re-identification:** Because white is normally reserved for the grounded conductor, it must be re-identified when used legally as an ungrounded conductor in a switch loop or similar arrangement.
   - **b. Black return conductor:** It carries switched line voltage from the switch to the luminaire so the light turns on when the switch closes.
   - **c. Neutral in the box:** It supports modern switching arrangements and makes future device replacement or control additions easier while following current wiring practice.
