## Glossary

This glossary uses a Canadian apprenticeship-prep perspective and aligns terminology with common Canadian Electrical Code (CEC) practice, trade usage, and introductory electrical theory.

## AFCI (Arc-Fault Circuit Interrupter)
An AFCI is a protective device designed to detect dangerous arcing conditions that may lead to fire, especially in residential branch circuits. It monitors the waveform for arc signatures and opens the circuit when abnormal arcing is detected. Standard trip action is based on internal detection algorithms rather than a single SI unit value.

## Alternating Current (AC)
Alternating current is electric current that reverses direction periodically, usually in a sinusoidal waveform for power systems. In Canada, common utility power is 60 hertz, meaning the waveform completes 60 cycles per second. Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz), and voltage is commonly expressed as RMS volts.

## American Wire Gauge (AWG)
AWG is a standardized conductor sizing system widely referenced in North American wiring practice. As the gauge number decreases, the conductor diameter and current-carrying capability generally increase. Conductor size is identified by AWG number or by larger sizes such as 1/0, 2/0, and so on.

## Ampacity
Ampacity is the maximum continuous current a conductor can carry without exceeding its allowable temperature rating under specified installation conditions. In Canadian practice, ampacity is selected from CEC tables and is affected by insulation type, ambient temperature, and conductor bundling. Ampacity is measured in amperes (A).

## Ampere (A)
The ampere is the SI unit of electric current and indicates the rate of charge flow through a conductor. One ampere corresponds to one coulomb of charge passing a point each second. The standard symbol is A.

## Apparent Power
Apparent power is the total power supplied in an AC circuit and combines real power and reactive power. It represents the product of RMS voltage and RMS current without considering phase angle. Apparent power is measured in volt-amperes (VA).

## Arc Flash
Arc flash is a dangerous release of thermal energy caused by an electric arc between energized conductors or between a conductor and ground. It can produce extreme temperatures, intense light, pressure waves, and molten metal, making PPE and safe work boundaries essential. Incident energy is often expressed in calories per square centimetre (cal/cm²).

## Armature
An armature is the winding or conductor arrangement in which voltage is induced or current interacts with a magnetic field to produce motion, depending on machine type. In motors and generators, the exact location of the armature depends on the construction of the machine. Relevant quantities include volts (V), amperes (A), and torque in newton-metres (N·m).

## Bonding
Bonding is the intentional electrical connection of non-current-carrying metal parts to maintain them at the same electrical potential. In Canadian installations, bonding reduces shock risk and supports effective fault-current return paths for rapid operation of overcurrent devices. Bonding conductors are sized according to code rules rather than a standalone SI unit.

## Branch Circuit
A branch circuit is the portion of the wiring system between the final overcurrent device and the connected outlets or loads. It supplies utilization equipment such as receptacles, lighting, or fixed appliances. Branch circuits are commonly identified by voltage and current ratings such as 120 V, 15 A or 20 A.

## Breaker
A breaker is a resettable overcurrent protective device that automatically opens a circuit when current exceeds a safe value or when a fault occurs. It replaces a fuse in many modern systems and is selected according to circuit voltage, ampacity, and application. Common ratings are expressed in amperes (A) and volts (V).

## Busbar
A busbar is a rigid conductor used inside switchboards, panelboards, and other distribution equipment to collect and distribute electrical power. Busbars are typically made of copper or aluminum and are designed for low impedance and high fault withstand capability. Ratings commonly include amperes (A), volts (V), and short-circuit current capacity.

## Capacitance
Capacitance is the ability of a component or system to store electric charge in an electric field. It increases with larger plate area, closer spacing, and higher dielectric constant. Capacitance is measured in farads (F), often expressed in microfarads (µF) or picofarads (pF).

## Capacitor
A capacitor is a component that stores energy in an electric field between conductive plates separated by an insulating material. It is used for filtering, timing, motor starting, and power-factor correction depending on the circuit. Its principal value is capacitance in farads (F), and its voltage rating must not be exceeded.

## Circuit
A circuit is a complete path that allows electric current to flow from a source, through a load, and back to the source. For current to flow, the circuit must be closed and have an applied potential difference. Important quantities include voltage (V), current (A), and resistance (Ω).

## Conductor
A conductor is a material that allows electric charge to move with relatively low resistance. Copper and aluminum are the most common building conductors because they combine conductivity with practical mechanical properties. Conductors are identified by size, material, insulation type, and ampacity.

## Conduit
Conduit is a raceway used to protect and route electrical conductors. It may be metallic or non-metallic and is selected based on environment, mechanical protection needs, and code requirements. Common trade sizes are stated in metric designators or legacy inch-based sizes.

## Continuity
Continuity means an unbroken electrical path exists between two points. Continuity testing is usually performed on de-energized circuits to verify conductors, switches, fuses, or connections. A good continuity reading typically shows very low resistance in ohms (Ω).

## Continuity Tester
A continuity tester is a device used to confirm that an electrical path is complete. Some testers provide a simple light or audible tone, while multimeters perform the same function with resistance or continuity mode. Continuity testing should be done only on de-energized circuits.

## Control Circuit
A control circuit is a circuit that governs the operation of another device or power circuit, often using relays, contactors, switches, and low-current signals. It allows safe and logical control of motors, heaters, and automated equipment. Control circuits are commonly rated by voltage such as 24 V, 120 V, or 600 V.

## Current
Current is the rate at which electric charge flows through a conductor or component. It is driven by voltage and limited by resistance or impedance in the circuit. Current is measured in amperes (A).

## De-energized
A de-energized condition exists when a circuit or piece of equipment has been disconnected from all sources of electrical energy and verified as not live. Proper de-energization includes isolation, lockout/tagout, and test-before-touch verification. Voltage should read 0 V on an approved tester before work begins.

## Dielectric
A dielectric is an insulating material that supports an electric field while resisting current flow. Dielectrics are used in capacitors and cable insulation because they influence breakdown strength and capacitance. Dielectric strength is commonly expressed in volts per millimetre (V/mm).

## Direct Current (DC)
Direct current is electric current that flows in one direction only. Batteries, photovoltaic modules, and many electronic power supplies produce DC. Voltage is expressed in volts (V), and there is no frequency reversal under steady DC conditions.

## Distribution Panel
A distribution panel, or panelboard, is an assembly that divides incoming electrical power into branch circuits protected by breakers or fuses. It contains busbars, neutral terminations, bonding arrangements, and overcurrent devices suited to the installation. Typical ratings include system voltage and main breaker current, such as 120/240 V, 100 A or 200 A.

## Earth Ground
Earth ground is the conductive connection between an electrical system and the earth. It helps stabilize system voltage to ground and supports fault management, although it is not meant to carry normal load current. Ground resistance is often measured in ohms (Ω) during grounding system testing.

## Electric Shock
Electric shock occurs when electric current passes through the human body. The severity depends on current magnitude, path through the body, duration, skin condition, and system voltage. Dangerous physiological effects can begin at relatively low current levels measured in milliamperes (mA).

## Electrical Energy
Electrical energy is the capacity to do work through the movement of electric charge over time. In practical billing and load calculations, electrical energy is tracked as power used over a duration. The common utility unit is the kilowatt-hour (kWh).

## Electrical Service
Electrical service is the complete set of conductors and equipment that delivers electrical energy from the utility supply point to the building distribution system. It includes service entrance conductors, the meter location, disconnecting means, and service equipment. Common Canadian residential services are rated 100 A or 200 A at 120/240 V.

## Electromagnetic Induction
Electromagnetic induction is the production of voltage in a conductor when the magnetic flux linking it changes. This principle is the foundation of transformers, generators, and many sensing devices. Induced voltage is measured in volts (V).

## Electron
An electron is a negatively charged subatomic particle involved in electric current in metallic conductors. While conventional current is described as flowing from positive to negative, electron flow is in the opposite direction. Charge is measured in coulombs (C).

## Fault Current
Fault current is the abnormal current that flows when an unintended low-impedance path develops, such as during a short circuit or ground fault. Protective devices and equipment interrupting ratings must be able to withstand and clear this current safely. Fault current is measured in amperes (A), often in kiloamperes (kA).

## Feeder
A feeder is a set of conductors that carries power from service equipment or distribution equipment to another distribution point, panel, or large load. Unlike a branch circuit, it usually supplies multiple downstream circuits or devices. Feeder size is determined by load and ampacity, measured in amperes (A).

## Frequency
Frequency is the number of complete waveform cycles that occur each second in an AC system. Utility frequency in Canada is 60 hertz. The SI unit is hertz (Hz).

## Fuse
A fuse is a sacrificial overcurrent protective device containing an element that melts when current exceeds a designed threshold. Once operated, it must be replaced with one of the correct type and rating. Fuses are rated in amperes (A), volts (V), and interrupting capacity.

## Ground Fault
A ground fault is an unintended connection between an energized conductor and ground or bonded metal parts. It can produce shock hazards, equipment damage, or fire if not cleared quickly. Ground-fault current is measured in amperes (A).

## Ground-Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI)
A GFCI is a protective device that disconnects a circuit when it detects a small imbalance between outgoing and returning current, indicating leakage to ground. It is commonly used in damp or wet locations to reduce shock risk. Class A devices typically trip at approximately 4 to 6 milliamperes (mA).

## Grounded Conductor
A grounded conductor is a system conductor intentionally connected to ground, usually the neutral in many AC systems. In Canadian wiring practice it is commonly identified with white insulation, though code rules and application details always govern. Its nominal voltage to ground may be near 0 V under normal conditions, but it is still a current-carrying conductor.

## Grounding
Grounding is the intentional connection of an electrical system or equipment to earth or to a reference ground point. Its purposes include voltage stabilization, fault management, and reduction of dangerous touch potentials. Grounding system performance is often assessed by resistance in ohms (Ω).

## Horsepower
Horsepower is a unit of mechanical power commonly used to rate motors. It indicates how much mechanical work the motor can perform over time. One horsepower is approximately 746 watts (W).

## Impedance
Impedance is the total opposition to AC, combining resistance and reactance. It determines how much current flows for a given AC voltage and depends on frequency as well as circuit elements. Impedance is measured in ohms (Ω).

## Inductor
An inductor is a component that stores energy in a magnetic field when current flows through a coil. It resists changes in current and is common in filters, transformers, and motor-related circuits. Inductance is measured in henries (H).

## Insulated Tools
Insulated tools are hand tools designed with protective handles or coatings to reduce the chance of accidental contact with energized parts. They are a risk-reduction measure, not a substitute for proper de-energization and safe work procedures. Tool ratings are commonly marked for maximum working voltage, such as 1000 V.

## Insulation
Insulation is non-conductive material applied around conductors to prevent unintended current flow and contact with live parts. Its selection depends on voltage, temperature, moisture, and mechanical conditions. Insulation ratings commonly include voltage class and temperature such as 300 V or 600 V, and 75°C or 90°C.

## Insulation Resistance
Insulation resistance is the resistance of insulating material to leakage current. High insulation resistance is generally desirable because it indicates low unwanted current flow between conductors or from conductor to ground. It is measured in ohms (Ω), often megohms (MΩ).

## Junction Box
A junction box is an enclosure used to contain conductor splices, terminations, or device connections while protecting them from mechanical damage and accidental contact. It also provides access for inspection and maintenance where permitted by code. Box size is chosen according to conductor fill and device requirements.

## Kilowatt-hour (kWh)
A kilowatt-hour is a unit of electrical energy equal to one kilowatt of power used for one hour. Utilities use it for billing because it represents actual energy consumption over time. One kilowatt-hour equals 3.6 megajoules (MJ).

## Line Voltage
Line voltage is the voltage measured between two line conductors in a polyphase or split-phase system. It differs from line-to-neutral voltage and is important in equipment selection and troubleshooting. It is measured in volts (V).

## Load
A load is any device or equipment that consumes electrical power to perform useful work, produce heat, generate light, or create motion. Loads may be resistive, inductive, capacitive, or a combination. Load is typically described by voltage, current, and power ratings such as 120 V, 12 A, or 1.5 kW.

## Locked-Rotor Current
Locked-rotor current is the high inrush current drawn by a motor when full voltage is applied and the rotor is not yet turning. It is much higher than normal running current and influences breaker, fuse, and starter selection. It is measured in amperes (A).

## Lockout/Tagout (LOTO)
Lockout/tagout is a safety procedure used to isolate energy sources and prevent accidental re-energization during maintenance or service work. A lock secures the isolating device, and a tag communicates who applied it and why. The process must include verification of zero energy before contact with conductors or components.

## Magnetic Field
A magnetic field is the region around a current-carrying conductor or magnet where magnetic forces can act. Magnetic fields are essential in transformers, inductors, relays, and motors. Magnetic flux density is measured in teslas (T).

## Megger
Megger is a common trade name often used generically for an insulation resistance tester. It applies a higher DC test voltage than a standard ohmmeter to evaluate insulation condition in cables, motors, and equipment. Test values are reported in megohms (MΩ), with common test voltages such as 250 V, 500 V, or 1000 V DC.

## Motor Starter
A motor starter is a control assembly that starts, stops, and protects a motor. It commonly includes a contactor for switching and overload protection for sustained overcurrent conditions. Ratings are typically given in volts (V), amperes (A), and horsepower (hp).

## Multimeter
A multimeter is a measuring instrument used to test voltage, current, resistance, and sometimes continuity, capacitance, or frequency. Proper lead placement and function selection are essential for safe and accurate measurements. Units depend on the mode selected, such as V, A, or Ω.

## Neutral
Neutral is the common conductor connected to the grounded point of a system, usually the center point of a transformer secondary or similar reference. In many Canadian branch circuits it is identified with white insulation and carries unbalanced return current under normal operation. Voltage between neutral and ground should be low under normal conditions but is not assumed to be zero in all cases.

## Ohm (Ω)
The ohm is the SI unit of electrical resistance or impedance. One ohm is the resistance through which one volt will produce one ampere of current. The standard symbol is Ω.

## Ohm's Law
Ohm's Law states that voltage equals current multiplied by resistance in a linear resistive circuit. It is one of the most important foundational relationships for basic electrical calculations and troubleshooting. The common forms are V = IR, I = V/R, and R = V/I.

## Open Circuit
An open circuit is a broken or incomplete path that prevents normal current flow. Open conditions may be caused by a switch position, a broken conductor, a failed component, or a loose termination. Current in an ideal open circuit is 0 A.

## Overcurrent
Overcurrent is any current exceeding the rated value of equipment or the ampacity of a conductor. It includes overload current, short-circuit current, and ground-fault current, depending on context. Overcurrent is measured in amperes (A).

## Overload
An overload is an overcurrent condition that occurs in an otherwise normal circuit without a fault path, usually because the load is too great or a motor is mechanically overburdened. Overloads develop over time and are typically handled by thermal protection. Overload current is measured in amperes (A).

## Parallel Circuit
A parallel circuit has two or more current paths connected across the same source voltage. Each branch sees the same voltage, while total current is the sum of the branch currents. Voltage is measured in volts (V), and branch currents are measured in amperes (A).

## Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)
PPE is the clothing and equipment worn to reduce exposure to workplace hazards such as shock, arc flash, impact, and flying debris. In electrical work this may include gloves, eye protection, face shields, and arc-rated garments. PPE selection is based on the hazard assessment and the task being performed.

## Phase
Phase describes the angular relationship of one AC waveform relative to another or relative to a reference. In power systems, phase relationships determine how voltages add, how motors rotate, and how power is delivered. Phase angle is commonly expressed in degrees (°).

## Phase Rotation
Phase rotation is the order in which the phases of a three-phase system reach their peak values. Correct rotation is important because it determines the direction of rotation for three-phase motors. Rotation is identified as a sequence such as A-B-C or L1-L2-L3.

## Polarity
Polarity identifies the relative positive and negative orientation of terminals in a DC circuit or the instantaneous relationship of terminals in certain AC components such as transformers. Correct polarity matters for proper device operation, safe testing, and correct wiring of control circuits. Voltage polarity is expressed in volts (V).

## Potential Difference
Potential difference is another term for voltage, meaning the difference in electric potential energy per unit charge between two points. It is the force that drives charge through a circuit. The SI unit is the volt (V).

## Power
Power is the rate at which electrical energy is transferred or converted. In electrical systems it is often calculated from voltage and current, with added phase-angle considerations in AC circuits. Real power is measured in watts (W).

## Power Factor
Power factor is the ratio of real power to apparent power in an AC circuit. It indicates how effectively current is being converted into useful work and is strongly influenced by phase angle in inductive or capacitive loads. Power factor is dimensionless and ranges from 0 to 1.

## Primary Winding
The primary winding is the input winding of a transformer, connected to the source side. It creates the magnetic flux in the core that enables voltage to be induced in the secondary winding. Relevant values include turns count, input voltage in volts (V), and current in amperes (A).

## Receptacle
A receptacle is a device that provides a connection point for a plug and cord to access a branch circuit. In Canadian residential work, common configurations include 15 A and 20 A receptacles on 125 V circuits. Device ratings are marked in volts (V) and amperes (A).

## Resistance
Resistance is the opposition to current flow in a conductor or component. It depends on material, length, cross-sectional area, and temperature. Resistance is measured in ohms (Ω).

## Resistor
A resistor is a component intentionally designed to provide resistance in a circuit. It is used to limit current, divide voltage, and shape signal or power behavior. Resistors are rated by resistance in ohms (Ω) and power dissipation in watts (W).

## Root Mean Square (RMS)
RMS is an effective value used to compare AC voltage or current to an equivalent DC value that would produce the same heating effect in a resistor. Standard utility voltages such as 120 V and 600 V are normally stated as RMS values. RMS voltage is measured in volts (V), and RMS current in amperes (A).

## Secondary Winding
The secondary winding is the output winding of a transformer, where voltage is induced by magnetic flux from the primary winding. Its voltage depends on the turns ratio between the windings. Output values are expressed in volts (V) and amperes (A).

## Series Circuit
A series circuit has only one path for current flow. The same current passes through each component, and the source voltage is divided among the loads according to their resistance or impedance. Current is measured in amperes (A), and voltage drops in volts (V).

## Service Entrance
Service entrance refers to the conductors and associated equipment that bring utility power into a building up to the service equipment. It must be installed to meet mechanical, weather, clearance, and code requirements. Typical residential service entrance systems are 120/240 V.

## Short Circuit
A short circuit is an unintended low-resistance path between conductors at different potentials. It causes very high fault current that can damage equipment and create fire or arc-flash hazards. Short-circuit current is measured in amperes (A), often kiloamperes (kA).

## Single-Phase
Single-phase describes an AC system in which one sinusoidal voltage is supplied, often with a grounded conductor reference. In residential Canadian systems, single-phase service commonly provides 120/240 V through a split-phase transformer arrangement. Frequency is typically 60 Hz.

## Slip
Slip is the difference between the synchronous speed of a motor's rotating magnetic field and the actual rotor speed in an induction motor. It is necessary for torque production in induction machines. Slip is commonly expressed as a percentage.

## Split-Phase System
A split-phase system is a single-phase transformer secondary with a center-tapped grounded conductor that provides two opposite-polarity line conductors. In Canadian homes this arrangement commonly delivers 120 V line-to-neutral and 240 V line-to-line. Voltage is measured in volts (V).

## Step-Down Transformer
A step-down transformer reduces voltage from primary to secondary by using fewer turns on the secondary winding. It is commonly used to convert distribution or control voltage to a lower usable level. Voltage values are measured in volts (V), and the ratio is determined by turns.

## Step-Up Transformer
A step-up transformer increases voltage from primary to secondary by using more turns on the secondary winding. It is used in power distribution and specialty applications where higher voltage is advantageous. Voltage is measured in volts (V), and the turns ratio determines the increase.

## Terminal
A terminal is a connection point where a conductor is mechanically and electrically joined to a device, component, or equipment assembly. Good terminal integrity is essential because loose or corroded terminations create resistance, heat, and intermittent faults. Tightening values may be specified as torque in newton-metres (N·m) or pound-inches.

## Test Light
A test light is a simple indicating device used to verify the presence of voltage. It is less informative than a multimeter but can provide quick field confirmation when used correctly and safely. Test lights must be suitable for the system voltage being checked.

## Thermal-Magnetic Breaker
A thermal-magnetic breaker uses two protective actions: a thermal element for sustained overloads and a magnetic element for rapid short-circuit response. This combination makes it suitable for many branch circuits and motor-related applications. Ratings commonly include amperes (A), volts (V), and interrupting capacity.

## Three-Phase
Three-phase describes an AC system with three sinusoidal voltages displaced by 120 electrical degrees. It delivers power efficiently and is widely used for motors, larger commercial services, and industrial distribution. Common Canadian system voltages include 208 V, 347/600 V, and 600 V equipment ratings.

## Torque
Torque is the turning force produced by a motor shaft or applied to a fastener. In motors, torque determines the ability to start and drive mechanical loads. The SI unit is the newton-metre (N·m).

## Transformer
A transformer is a static electromagnetic device that transfers electrical energy between circuits through mutual induction. It changes voltage and current levels while ideally maintaining the same frequency. Ratings commonly include volts (V), amperes (A), volt-amperes (VA), or kilovolt-amperes (kVA).

## Troubleshooting
Troubleshooting is the structured process of identifying, isolating, and correcting electrical faults. Good troubleshooting relies on safe work practices, accurate measurements, knowledge of circuit operation, and verification after repair. Typical measured quantities include volts (V), amperes (A), ohms (Ω), and continuity.

## Ungrounded Conductor
An ungrounded conductor is a live conductor that is not intentionally connected to ground. In common Canadian branch-circuit wiring it is often identified by black or red insulation, depending on the circuit arrangement. Its voltage is measured relative to neutral or ground in volts (V).

## Voltage
Voltage is the electrical potential difference that drives current through a circuit. It can be thought of as electrical pressure between two points. The SI unit is the volt (V).

## Voltage Drop
Voltage drop is the reduction in voltage along a conductor due to current flow through its resistance or impedance. Excessive voltage drop can cause poor equipment performance, overheating, dim lighting, or motor starting problems. It is measured in volts (V) and often expressed as a percentage of system voltage.

## Watt
The watt is the SI unit of power. One watt equals one joule of energy transferred or converted each second. Electrical power in watts is commonly calculated as volts multiplied by amperes under DC or purely resistive AC conditions.

## Watt-hour
A watt-hour is a unit of energy equal to one watt of power used for one hour. It is smaller than the kilowatt-hour used for utility billing but follows the same principle of power multiplied by time. One watt-hour equals 3600 joules (J).

## Winding
A winding is a coil of conductor arranged to create or link magnetic flux in transformers, motors, generators, and inductors. The number of turns, conductor size, and insulation system determine its electrical performance. Winding values are associated with volts (V), amperes (A), resistance (Ω), and turns count.

## Work
In electrical terms, work is the transfer of energy when electric charge moves through a potential difference. It is closely related to energy consumption and power over time. The SI unit of work is the joule (J).
